# Diabetes Management Guidelines

Source: ADA Standards of Care 2026, NIDDK, CDC

---

## Blood Glucose Monitoring

### Target Ranges (General)
- Before meals (preprandial): 80-130 mg/dL
- 2 hours after meals (postprandial): <180 mg/dL
- A1C target: <7% for most adults
- Time in range (70-180 mg/dL): at least 70% of the day
- Time below range (<70 mg/dL): less than 4% of the day
- Time below range (<54 mg/dL): less than 1% of the day

### Adjusted Targets
- Older adults (65+) or those with significant comorbidities: A1C <8%
- Pregnant women: more stringent targets (fasting <95, 1-hour <140, 2-hour <120 mg/dL)
- Children and adolescents: A1C <7% (individualized)

### Monitoring Methods
- Self-monitoring blood glucose (SMBG): fingerstick with glucose meter
- Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM): sensor under skin, real-time readings
- A1C testing: every 3 months (or at least twice yearly if stable)

## The ABCs of Diabetes Care

### A — A1C
- Test every 3 months or at minimum twice yearly
- Target <7% for most adults
- Each 1% reduction in A1C reduces microvascular complications by ~25%

### B — Blood Pressure
- Target: <130/80 mmHg for most adults with diabetes
- Higher targets may be appropriate for some elderly patients
- First-line medications: ACE inhibitors or ARBs (especially with kidney disease)
- Lifestyle: sodium restriction (<2,300 mg/day), weight management, exercise

### C — Cholesterol
- Statin therapy recommended for most adults with diabetes aged 40-75
- LDL target depends on cardiovascular risk:
  - High risk (existing CVD): LDL <70 mg/dL
  - Moderate risk: LDL <100 mg/dL
- HDL goal: >40 mg/dL (men), >50 mg/dL (women)
- Triglycerides goal: <150 mg/dL

### S — Stop Smoking
- Smoking cessation is critical — both conditions narrow blood vessels
- Include e-cigarettes and vaping
- Offer counseling and pharmacotherapy for cessation

## Medication Management

### Type 2 Diabetes Medication Progression

**First-line:** Metformin
- Reduces hepatic glucose production
- Improves insulin sensitivity
- Weight neutral or slight weight loss
- Low hypoglycemia risk
- Contraindicated in severe renal impairment (eGFR <30)

**Second-line (add if A1C still above target):**
- SGLT-2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin) — cardiovascular and renal benefits
- GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide) — weight loss, CV benefit
- DPP-4 inhibitors (sitagliptin, linagliptin) — weight neutral
- Sulfonylureas (glimepiride, glipizide) — low cost but hypoglycemia risk
- Thiazolidinediones (pioglitazone) — fluid retention risk

**Third-line:** Insulin therapy
- Basal insulin (glargine, detemir, degludec)
- Basal-bolus insulin (add rapid-acting at meals)
- Premixed insulin formulations

### Special Considerations
- With established cardiovascular disease: prefer SGLT-2 inhibitors or GLP-1 agonists
- With heart failure: prefer SGLT-2 inhibitors
- With chronic kidney disease: prefer SGLT-2 inhibitors or GLP-1 agonists
- Cost-sensitive: metformin + sulfonylurea + NPH insulin

## Lifestyle Management

### Nutrition
- No single ideal diet — individualize with registered dietitian
- Emphasize non-starchy vegetables, whole grains, lean proteins
- Limit added sugars, refined grains, and processed foods
- Consistent carbohydrate intake helps with glucose management
- Moderate alcohol consumption (if any): <=1 drink/day women, <=2 drinks/day men
- Always eat when drinking alcohol to prevent hypoglycemia

### Physical Activity
- 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity (brisk walking)
- OR 75 minutes per week of vigorous activity
- Resistance training: 2-3 sessions per week
- Reduce prolonged sedentary time (break up every 30 minutes)
- Check blood glucose before, during, and after exercise
- Carry fast-acting carbohydrate during exercise

### Weight Management
- 5-7% body weight loss significantly improves glycemic control
- Sustained weight loss of 5% improves A1C, blood pressure, lipids
- Consider metabolic surgery for BMI >= 40 (or >= 35 with diabetes)

### Sleep
- Target 7-8 hours per night
- Screen for and treat obstructive sleep apnea (common in type 2 diabetes)
- Poor sleep worsens insulin resistance and appetite regulation

## Sick Day Management

### General Rules
- NEVER stop insulin (especially type 1 diabetes)
- Monitor blood glucose more frequently (every 2-4 hours)
- Check ketones if blood glucose >250 mg/dL (type 1) or if feeling ill
- Stay hydrated — drink small sips of water or sugar-free fluids
- Try to maintain normal meal schedule
- Contact healthcare provider if:
  - Blood glucose consistently >250 mg/dL
  - Ketones are moderate to large
  - Vomiting persists >4 hours
  - Unable to keep fluids down
  - Fever >101°F (38.3°C) persisting >24 hours

### Medication Adjustments During Illness
- Continue metformin unless dehydrated or vomiting
- Hold SGLT-2 inhibitors during acute illness (DKA risk)
- Insulin doses may need increase (illness causes stress hormones to rise)
- Never reduce insulin dose without healthcare guidance

## When to Seek Medical Care

### Routine (GREEN — Schedule Appointment)
- A1C above target but stable
- Mild, manageable symptoms
- Medication refill needed
- Routine screening due

### Urgent (YELLOW — See Doctor Within 24-48 Hours)
- Blood glucose consistently >250 mg/dL despite medication
- New or worsening numbness/tingling
- Non-healing wound or sore (especially on feet)
- Recurrent infections
- Significant weight change without explanation
- New vision changes

### Emergency (RED — Seek Immediate Care)
- Blood glucose >300 mg/dL with symptoms
- Blood glucose <54 mg/dL not responding to treatment
- Fruity breath odor
- Severe nausea/vomiting with high blood sugar
- Confusion or altered mental status
- Loss of consciousness
- Seizure
- Chest pain
- Difficulty breathing
- Signs of DKA or HHS
