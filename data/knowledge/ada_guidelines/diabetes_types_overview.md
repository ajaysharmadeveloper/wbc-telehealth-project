# Diabetes Types — Clinical Overview

Source: ADA Standards of Care 2026, CDC, WHO, NIDDK

---

## Type 1 Diabetes

### Definition
Autoimmune condition where the body's immune system attacks and destroys insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas, resulting in absolute insulin deficiency.

### Epidemiology
- Accounts for 5-10% of all diabetes cases
- Usually diagnosed in children and young adults, but can occur at any age
- 9 million people affected globally
- Incidence increasing ~3% per year worldwide

### Pathophysiology
- Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells
- Genetic susceptibility (HLA-DR3, HLA-DR4) combined with environmental triggers
- Autoantibodies present: anti-GAD65, anti-IA-2, anti-insulin, anti-ZnT8
- Progressive beta-cell loss leads to absolute insulin deficiency

### Symptoms
- Onset is often rapid (days to weeks)
- Polyuria (frequent urination)
- Polydipsia (excessive thirst)
- Polyphagia (excessive hunger) with unexplained weight loss
- Fatigue
- Blurred vision
- May present initially as DKA (especially in children)
- In children: bed-wetting, increased bathroom trips

### Management
- Lifelong insulin therapy required (no alternative)
- Multiple daily injections (MDI) or insulin pump therapy
- Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) recommended
- Carbohydrate counting
- Regular A1C testing (target typically <7%)
- Cannot be prevented with current knowledge

## Type 2 Diabetes

### Definition
Progressive condition where the body becomes resistant to insulin and/or the pancreas cannot produce enough insulin to maintain normal blood glucose levels.

### Epidemiology
- Accounts for 90-95% of all diabetes cases
- Over 830 million adults affected globally (2022)
- Prevalence doubled in last 20 years
- Increasingly diagnosed in children and adolescents
- More than 1 in 4 adults with diabetes are undiagnosed

### Pathophysiology
- Insulin resistance: cells in muscles, fat, and liver don't respond effectively to insulin
- Progressive beta-cell dysfunction: pancreas produces less insulin over time
- Hepatic glucose overproduction
- Often associated with metabolic syndrome (obesity, hypertension, dyslipidemia)

### Risk Factors
- Overweight or obesity (especially abdominal/visceral fat)
- Age >= 35 (risk increases with age)
- Family history of type 2 diabetes (first-degree relative)
- High-risk ethnicity: African American, Hispanic/Latino, Native American, Asian American, Pacific Islander
- Physical inactivity
- History of gestational diabetes
- Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)
- Hypertension (>= 140/90 mmHg)
- Dyslipidemia (HDL <35 mg/dL, triglycerides >250 mg/dL)
- Prediabetes (A1C 5.7-6.4%)
- History of cardiovascular disease
- Smoking
- Sleep disorders (obstructive sleep apnea)

### Symptoms
- Develop gradually over years
- Many patients are asymptomatic initially
- Classic symptoms when present: polyuria, polydipsia, fatigue
- Additional signs: slow-healing wounds, frequent infections, numbness/tingling in extremities
- Dark skin patches (acanthosis nigricans) around neck, armpits, groin

### Management
- Lifestyle modifications: diet, exercise, weight management
- First-line medication: metformin
- Additional medications as needed: SGLT-2 inhibitors, GLP-1 receptor agonists, sulfonylureas, DPP-4 inhibitors
- Insulin therapy when oral medications insufficient
- Blood pressure and cholesterol management
- Regular screening for complications

### Prevention
- 150+ minutes per week of moderate physical activity
- Healthy diet (reduce sugar and saturated fat)
- Weight loss of 5-7% body weight if overweight
- Avoid tobacco
- Regular screening if at risk

## Gestational Diabetes

### Definition
Diabetes first diagnosed during the second or third trimester of pregnancy that is not clearly pre-existing type 1 or type 2 diabetes.

### Epidemiology
- Affects 2-10% of pregnancies annually
- Risk of developing type 2 diabetes after pregnancy: 50% within 5-10 years

### Risk Factors
- Overweight or obesity before pregnancy
- Age over 25
- Family history of diabetes
- Previous gestational diabetes
- Previous birth of baby weighing >9 pounds (4.1 kg)
- High-risk ethnicity
- PCOS
- Physical inactivity

### Screening
- All pregnant women should be tested at 24-28 weeks gestation
- Women with risk factors: test at first prenatal visit
- Postpartum: test at 4-12 weeks after delivery
- Lifelong screening every 1-3 years for women with history of GDM

### Symptoms
- Usually no symptoms (detected through routine screening)
- When present: increased thirst, frequent urination (mild)

### Impact on Baby
- Increased risk of macrosomia (large baby)
- Neonatal hypoglycemia
- Childhood obesity
- Increased future risk of type 2 diabetes

### Management
- Diet modification and meal planning
- Physical activity (moderate intensity)
- Blood glucose monitoring
- Insulin therapy if lifestyle changes insufficient
- Metformin or glyburide may be used in some cases

## Prediabetes

### Definition
Blood glucose levels higher than normal but not yet high enough for type 2 diabetes diagnosis. A critical window for intervention.

### Diagnostic Criteria
- A1C: 5.7-6.4%
- Fasting glucose: 100-125 mg/dL (impaired fasting glucose)
- OGTT 2-hour glucose: 140-199 mg/dL (impaired glucose tolerance)

### Epidemiology
- Over 98 million US adults have prediabetes (38% of adult population)
- More than 80% don't know they have it
- Without intervention: 15-30% develop type 2 diabetes within 5 years

### Intervention
- Structured lifestyle modification program (DPP model)
- 5-7% body weight loss
- 150 minutes per week of moderate physical activity
- Annual monitoring of blood glucose
- Metformin may be considered for high-risk individuals
