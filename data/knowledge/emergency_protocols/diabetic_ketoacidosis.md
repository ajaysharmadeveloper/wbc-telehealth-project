# Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

Source: NIH StatPearls, ADA Consensus Report 2024, NIH Endotext

---

## Definition
DKA is a serious, potentially life-threatening complication of diabetes characterized by hyperglycemia, metabolic acidosis, and ketonemia. While strongly associated with type 1 diabetes, it can also occur in type 2 diabetes under certain conditions.

## Diagnostic Criteria
- Blood glucose: >250 mg/dL (exceptions: euglycemic DKA can occur below 250 mg/dL)
- Arterial pH: <7.3
- Serum bicarbonate: <15 mEq/L (mild: 15-18, moderate: 10-15, severe: <10)
- Ketonemia or ketonuria: present (venous beta-hydroxybutyrate >= 3.0 mmol/L)
- Anion gap: >14-15 mEq/L (high anion gap metabolic acidosis)

## Classification by Severity

| Feature | Mild | Moderate | Severe |
|---------|------|----------|--------|
| Blood glucose | >250 mg/dL | >250 mg/dL | >250 mg/dL |
| Arterial pH | 7.25-7.30 | 7.0-7.24 | <7.0 |
| Bicarbonate | 15-18 mEq/L | 10-14.9 mEq/L | <10 mEq/L |
| Mental status | Alert | Alert/drowsy | Stupor/coma |
| Anion gap | >10 | >12 | >12 |

## Signs and Symptoms

### Early Symptoms
- Polyuria (frequent urination)
- Polydipsia (excessive thirst)
- Polyphagia (excessive hunger)
- Fatigue and weakness
- Nausea and vomiting
- Abdominal pain

### Progressive Symptoms
- Kussmaul breathing (labored, deep, rapid respirations)
- Fruity/acetone breath odor
- Dehydration (dry mouth, decreased sweating, reduced urine)
- Tachycardia and tachypnea
- Hypotension (in severe cases)

### Severe/Emergency Symptoms
- Altered mental status, confusion
- Loss of consciousness
- Seizures
- Cerebral edema (especially in children)

## Common Precipitating Factors
- New-onset diabetes (first presentation)
- Infection (pneumonia, UTIs are most common triggers)
- Insulin non-adherence or omission
- Acute medical illness (myocardial infarction, stroke, trauma)
- Medications: corticosteroids, SGLT-2 inhibitors, antipsychotics, thiazides
- Substance use (cocaine, cannabis)
- Emotional stress
- Surgery

## Special Consideration: Euglycemic DKA
- High anion gap metabolic acidosis with positive ketones despite glucose <250 mg/dL
- Commonly seen with SGLT-2 inhibitor use or GLP-1 agonists
- Can occur in pregnancy (up to 30% of pregnancy-related DKA)

## Treatment Protocol

### Fluid Resuscitation
- Initial: 15-20 mL/kg during first hour with isotonic crystalloid (0.9% NaCl)
- Maintenance: 4-14 mL/kg/hr adjusted based on hemodynamics and sodium levels
- Typical fluid deficit: 10-15% of body weight
- Add 5-10% dextrose when glucose reaches 200-250 mg/dL

### Insulin Therapy
- Standard: Continuous IV infusion at 0.14 U/kg/hr (no bolus needed)
- Alternative: 0.1 U/kg bolus followed by 0.1 U/kg/hr infusion
- CRITICAL: Only initiate when serum potassium >= 3.5 mmol/L
- Reduce to 0.05 U/kg/hr when glucose reaches 200-250 mg/dL with persistent anion gap
- Mild DKA: Subcutaneous insulin lispro 0.1 U/kg hourly (non-ICU)

### Electrolyte Management
- Potassium: Start replacement when <5.2 mEq/L; 20-30 mEq per liter fluid
- Delay insulin if potassium <3.5 mmol/L (cardiac arrhythmia risk)
- Magnesium: Often low; repletion may be needed before potassium normalizes
- Bicarbonate: Only recommended when pH <7.1 (controversial, may worsen outcomes)

### Resolution Criteria (ALL must be met)
- Blood glucose <200 mg/dL
- Serum bicarbonate >= 15 mEq/L
- Venous pH >7.3
- Calculated anion gap <= 12 mEq/L

## Mortality
- 0.2-2.5% in developing countries
- ~1% with modern management
- Higher risk: nursing home residents, extremes of age, concurrent illness, coma, hypotension

## Triage Level: RED (Emergency)
Any patient presenting with signs/symptoms of DKA requires immediate emergency medical care. This is a medical emergency — do NOT attempt to manage at home.
