# Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS)

Source: NIH Endotext, ADA Consensus Report

---

## Definition
HHS is a life-threatening complication of diabetes characterized by severe hyperglycemia, hyperosmolality, and dehydration WITHOUT significant ketoacidosis. It primarily affects patients with type 2 diabetes and has a mortality rate of 10-20% (approximately 10 times higher than DKA).

## Diagnostic Criteria (ALL required)
- Plasma glucose: >600 mg/dL
- Effective serum osmolality: >300 mOsm/kg (or total >320 mOsm/kg)
- Absent significant ketonemia: beta-hydroxybutyrate <3.0 mmol/L
- No acidosis: pH >= 7.3 AND bicarbonate >= 15 mmol/L

## DKA vs HHS Comparison

| Feature | DKA | HHS |
|---------|-----|-----|
| Onset | Rapid (hours to 2 days) | Gradual (days to weeks) |
| Blood glucose | 200-600 mg/dL | >600 mg/dL (often >1000) |
| Ketones | >3 mmol/L | <3 mmol/L |
| pH | <7.3 | >= 7.3 |
| Osmolality | Moderately elevated | Severely elevated (>320) |
| Mental status | Mild-moderate confusion | Severe confusion/seizures/coma |
| Typical diabetes type | Type 1 | Type 2 |
| Dehydration | Moderate (10-15%) | Severe (15-25%) |

## Signs and Symptoms

### Early Signs
- Profound fatigue and weakness
- Excessive thirst (polydipsia)
- Frequent urination (polyuria)
- Gradual weight loss

### Progressive Symptoms
- Severe dehydration
- Dry mucous membranes
- Poor skin turgor
- Tachycardia
- Hypotension

### Severe/Emergency Symptoms
- Severe confusion and disorientation
- Seizures
- Focal neurological deficits
- Coma
- Altered mental status correlates with degree of hyperosmolality

## Precipitating Factors
- Infection (40-60% of cases — pneumonia, UTI most common)
- Inadequate insulin therapy or medication non-adherence
- New-onset type 2 diabetes (undiagnosed)
- Acute illness: myocardial infarction, stroke, pancreatitis
- Medications: glucocorticoids, thiazide diuretics, beta-blockers
- Impaired access to water (elderly, nursing home residents, cognitive impairment)
- Renal insufficiency

## Treatment Protocol

### Fluid Resuscitation (MOST CRITICAL)
- Replace 50% of estimated fluid deficit in first 12 hours
- Remainder over the following 12 hours
- Preferred: balanced crystalloids (Ringer's lactate) over normal saline
- Add 5-10% dextrose when glucose <250 mg/dL
- Typical deficit: 8-12 liters

### Insulin Therapy
- Initiate at 0.05 units/kg/hr (LOWER than DKA — 0.1 units/kg/hr)
- Start ONLY after glucose has plateaued with fluid therapy alone
- Rapid insulin dosing risks osmotic shifts and cerebral edema
- Continue until resolution criteria met

### Resolution Criteria
- Serum osmolality <300 mOsm/kg
- Blood glucose <250 mg/dL
- Urine output >0.5 mL/kg/hr
- Improved cognitive status

## Epidemiology
- Incidence: 1.2 per 1,000 hospital admissions
- Accounts for <1% of all diabetes-related hospital admissions
- Mortality: 10-20% (much higher than DKA)
- Inpatient care cost (US): ~$31,000 per admission
- Most common in elderly patients with type 2 diabetes

## Complications
- Thrombosis (prothrombotic state)
- Cerebral edema (from overly aggressive fluid/insulin therapy)
- Acute kidney injury
- Rhabdomyolysis
- Pulmonary edema

## Triage Level: RED (Emergency)
Any patient presenting with signs/symptoms of HHS requires immediate emergency medical care. The high mortality rate makes early recognition and treatment critical.
