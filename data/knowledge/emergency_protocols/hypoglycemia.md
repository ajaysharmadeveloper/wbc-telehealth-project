# Hypoglycemia (Low Blood Sugar)

Source: ADA Standards of Care 2026, NIDDK, CDC

---

## Definition
Hypoglycemia occurs when blood glucose drops below normal levels. For most people with diabetes, blood glucose below 70 mg/dL is considered low. Severe hypoglycemia is a medical emergency.

## Classification

| Level | Blood Glucose | Description |
|-------|--------------|-------------|
| Level 1 (Alert) | <70 mg/dL (3.9 mmol/L) | Sufficiently low for treatment with fast-acting carbohydrate |
| Level 2 (Clinically significant) | <54 mg/dL (3.0 mmol/L) | Sufficiently low to indicate serious, clinically important hypoglycemia |
| Level 3 (Severe) | No specific threshold | Requires assistance of another person; may involve altered mental status, seizure, or loss of consciousness |

## Signs and Symptoms

### Mild to Moderate Symptoms
- Shakiness or trembling
- Sweating (diaphoresis)
- Rapid heartbeat (tachycardia)
- Feeling anxious or nervous
- Irritability or mood changes
- Dizziness or lightheadedness
- Hunger
- Pale skin color
- Tingling or numbness in lips, tongue, or cheeks
- Headache
- Blurred or impaired vision
- Difficulty concentrating
- Weakness and fatigue

### Severe Symptoms (EMERGENCY)
- Confusion or disorientation
- Unusual behavior, appearing drunk
- Inability to eat or drink
- Slurred speech
- Blurred or double vision
- Drowsiness
- Seizures or convulsions
- Loss of consciousness
- Coma

## Causes
- Too much insulin or diabetes medication (sulfonylureas, meglitinides)
- Skipping or delaying meals
- Not eating enough carbohydrates
- Unplanned or excessive physical activity
- Drinking alcohol without eating
- Illness, especially with vomiting or diarrhea
- Hormonal deficiencies
- Certain medications (beta-blockers may mask symptoms)

## Treatment

### Mild to Moderate (Patient Can Self-Treat)
**Rule of 15:**
1. Eat or drink 15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrate:
   - 4 glucose tablets
   - 4 ounces (1/2 cup) of fruit juice or regular soda
   - 1 tablespoon of sugar or honey
   - Hard candies (check label for amount)
2. Wait 15 minutes
3. Recheck blood glucose
4. If still below 70 mg/dL, repeat treatment
5. Once glucose is back to normal, eat a snack or meal within 30 minutes

### Severe Hypoglycemia (EMERGENCY)
- Patient cannot self-treat — requires assistance
- Administer glucagon (injection or nasal spray)
- Call emergency services (911)
- Do NOT put food or liquids in the mouth of an unconscious person
- Place person on their side (recovery position)
- Once conscious and able to swallow, give fast-acting carbohydrates

## Risk Factors for Hypoglycemia
- Insulin therapy (highest risk)
- Sulfonylurea or meglitinide use
- Older age
- Renal impairment (reduced insulin clearance)
- Hepatic impairment
- Long duration of diabetes
- History of previous severe hypoglycemia
- Hypoglycemia unawareness (reduced warning symptoms over time)
- Cognitive impairment
- Excessive alcohol intake
- Irregular eating patterns

## Hypoglycemia Unawareness
- Loss of warning symptoms that normally accompany low blood sugar
- Occurs more frequently in those with:
  - Long-standing type 1 diabetes
  - Frequent hypoglycemic episodes
  - Tight glycemic control (low A1C targets)
- Prevention: Strict avoidance of hypoglycemia for several weeks can restore awareness
- CGM (continuous glucose monitoring) is strongly recommended

## When to Seek Emergency Care
- Blood glucose below 54 mg/dL that doesn't respond to treatment
- Loss of consciousness
- Seizure
- Unable to eat or drink
- Confusion that doesn't resolve after treatment
- Repeated episodes of severe hypoglycemia

## Prevention
- Regular blood glucose monitoring
- Consistent meal timing and carbohydrate intake
- Adjusting medication doses with healthcare provider guidance
- Carrying fast-acting glucose at all times
- Wearing medical identification
- Educating family/friends on glucagon administration
- Using CGM with low-glucose alerts
- Adjusting insulin before exercise

## Triage Levels
- **GREEN**: Mild hypoglycemia (>54 mg/dL), patient can self-treat, responsive, no confusion
- **YELLOW**: Moderate hypoglycemia, recurrent episodes, difficulty self-managing
- **RED**: Severe hypoglycemia (<54 mg/dL), loss of consciousness, seizure, unable to self-treat
