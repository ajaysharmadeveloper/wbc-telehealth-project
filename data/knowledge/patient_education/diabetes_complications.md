# Diabetes Complications — What Patients Should Know

Source: NIDDK, CDC, WHO, ADA

---

## Why Complications Happen

High blood sugar over time damages blood vessels and nerves throughout your body. This damage can affect your heart, eyes, kidneys, nerves, feet, teeth, and more. The good news: managing your blood sugar, blood pressure, and cholesterol significantly reduces the risk of complications.

---

## Cardiovascular Disease (Heart and Blood Vessels)

### The Risk
- Adults with diabetes are 2-4 times more likely to die from heart disease
- Diabetes is a major risk factor for heart attack and stroke
- High blood sugar damages blood vessels and nerves that control the heart

### Warning Signs
- Chest pain or pressure (may be atypical in diabetes — watch for jaw, arm, or back pain)
- Shortness of breath
- Swelling in legs or feet
- Dizziness or fainting

### Prevention
- Keep A1C, blood pressure, and cholesterol at target
- Take medications as prescribed (statins, blood pressure meds)
- Don't smoke
- Stay physically active
- Maintain healthy weight

### Triage: Chest pain with diabetes = RED (emergency)

## Diabetic Neuropathy (Nerve Damage)

### Types

**Peripheral neuropathy** (most common)
- Affects feet and legs first, then hands and arms
- Symptoms: numbness, tingling, burning, sharp pain, sensitivity to touch
- Can lead to inability to feel injuries, increasing infection risk

**Autonomic neuropathy**
- Affects internal organs
- Symptoms: digestive problems (gastroparesis), bladder issues, sexual dysfunction
- Can cause unrecognized hypoglycemia (hypoglycemia unawareness)
- May cause abnormal heart rate or blood pressure responses

**Focal neuropathy**
- Sudden weakness of one nerve or group of nerves
- Can cause muscle weakness, pain, double vision

### Warning Signs — See a Doctor
- New or worsening numbness/tingling in hands or feet
- Sharp, burning, or shooting pain
- Muscle weakness
- Digestive problems (nausea, bloating, constipation, diarrhea)
- Dizziness when standing up
- Changes in urination patterns

### Prevention
- Maintain blood glucose targets
- Check feet daily for cuts, blisters, sores
- Annual comprehensive foot exam

## Diabetic Nephropathy (Kidney Disease)

### The Risk
- Diabetes is the #1 cause of kidney failure
- Affects ~1 in 3 adults with diabetes
- Often has no symptoms until advanced stages

### Stages
1. **Early (microalbuminuria):** Small amounts of protein in urine; no symptoms
2. **Moderate:** Increasing protein in urine; may develop swelling
3. **Severe:** Kidney function declining; may need dialysis or transplant

### Warning Signs
- Swelling in ankles, feet, or hands (edema)
- Increased or decreased urination
- Fatigue
- Nausea
- Itchy skin
- Foamy or bubbly urine

### Screening
- Annual urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR) test
- Annual serum creatinine and eGFR

### Prevention
- Control blood sugar and blood pressure
- ACE inhibitors or ARBs recommended (even without hypertension if albuminuria present)
- Limit sodium intake
- Don't smoke

## Diabetic Retinopathy (Eye Disease)

### The Risk
- Diabetes is the #1 cause of new blindness in adults aged 20-74
- Affects blood vessels in the retina
- Often no early symptoms — damage happens before vision changes

### Types
- **Non-proliferative (early):** Small blood vessel changes, microaneurysms, may leak fluid
- **Proliferative (advanced):** New abnormal blood vessels grow; risk of retinal detachment and vision loss

### Other Eye Conditions
- **Diabetic macular edema:** Swelling in the central vision area
- **Cataracts:** 2-5 times more common in people with diabetes
- **Glaucoma:** Nearly double the risk

### Warning Signs — See Eye Doctor Immediately
- Sudden blurry or distorted vision
- Floaters (spots or dark strings in vision)
- Flashes of light
- Dark or empty areas in vision
- Difficulty seeing at night
- Vision loss

### Screening
- Comprehensive dilated eye exam annually (type 2: at diagnosis; type 1: within 5 years of diagnosis)
- More frequent if retinopathy already present

### Prevention
- Maintain blood sugar, blood pressure, and cholesterol targets
- Don't smoke
- Annual eye exams

## Diabetic Foot Problems

### The Risk
- Nerve damage + poor circulation = reduced ability to feel and heal foot injuries
- Diabetes is the leading cause of non-traumatic lower-limb amputations
- Small cuts or blisters can become serious infections

### Warning Signs — See a Doctor
- Sores or wounds that don't heal within a few days
- Redness, warmth, or swelling
- Calluses with bleeding underneath
- Ingrown toenails with signs of infection
- Foot shape changes
- Loss of feeling in feet
- Pain in legs when walking (claudication)

### Foot Care Basics
- Check feet daily (top, bottom, between toes)
- Wash feet daily in warm (not hot) water; dry thoroughly
- Never walk barefoot
- Wear well-fitting shoes and clean socks
- Cut toenails straight across
- Don't try to remove calluses yourself
- Annual comprehensive foot exam by healthcare provider

### Emergency: Foot infection with fever, red streaks, or pus = YELLOW to RED

## Dental and Gum Disease

### The Risk
- High blood sugar feeds bacteria in the mouth
- Increased risk of gum disease (periodontitis)
- Gum disease can make blood sugar harder to control (bidirectional)

### Warning Signs
- Red, swollen, or bleeding gums
- Loose teeth
- Bad breath that won't go away
- Changes in how teeth fit together

### Prevention
- Brush twice daily, floss daily
- Regular dental checkups (at least every 6 months)
- Maintain blood sugar targets
- Don't smoke

## Sexual and Bladder Problems

### In Men
- Erectile dysfunction (ED) — affects up to 75% of men with diabetes
- Retrograde ejaculation
- Low testosterone

### In Women
- Decreased sexual desire
- Vaginal dryness
- Pain during intercourse
- Frequent urinary tract infections

### Bladder Issues (Both)
- Overactive bladder
- Difficulty fully emptying bladder
- Urinary incontinence

### When to Discuss with Doctor
- These are common and treatable
- Don't feel embarrassed — your healthcare team can help
- Treatments available for most conditions

## Global Impact Statistics (WHO)
- Diabetes caused 1.6 million deaths directly in 2021
- 47% of diabetes deaths occur before age 70
- Diabetes-related kidney disease caused 530,000 additional deaths
- High blood glucose contributes to ~11% of cardiovascular deaths
- Total US healthcare costs: $640 billion annually (25% of all healthcare spending)

---

## Key Prevention Message

The single most important thing you can do to prevent complications: **keep your blood sugar, blood pressure, and cholesterol at your target levels.** Work with your healthcare team, take your medications as prescribed, eat well, stay active, and attend all recommended screening appointments.
