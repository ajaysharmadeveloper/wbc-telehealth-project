# Diabetes Self-Care Guide

Source: ADA, NIDDK, CDC

---

## Daily Self-Care Checklist

### Blood Sugar Management
- Check blood glucose as recommended by your healthcare provider
- Take all medications on time and as prescribed
- Track your numbers in a log or app
- Know your target ranges:
  - Before meals: 80-130 mg/dL
  - 2 hours after meals: <180 mg/dL
  - A1C: <7% (or your individualized target)

### Healthy Eating
- Eat regular meals — don't skip meals
- Fill half your plate with non-starchy vegetables
- Choose whole grains over refined grains
- Include lean protein with each meal
- Limit sugary drinks — choose water, unsweetened tea, or coffee
- Limit saturated fat and sodium
- If you drink alcohol, do so in moderation and always with food
- Work with a registered dietitian for personalized guidance

### Physical Activity
- Aim for 150 minutes per week of moderate activity (brisk walking, swimming, cycling)
- Include strength training 2-3 times per week
- Break up long periods of sitting every 30 minutes
- Check blood sugar before and after exercise
- Carry fast-acting glucose during activity
- Stay hydrated

### Foot Care
- Inspect your feet daily (use a mirror for bottoms)
- Wash feet daily; dry thoroughly, especially between toes
- Apply moisturizer (not between toes)
- Never walk barefoot
- Wear comfortable, well-fitting shoes
- Trim toenails straight across
- Report any sores, blisters, or changes to your doctor

### Mental Health
- Diabetes distress is common — it's okay to feel overwhelmed
- Talk to your healthcare team if you're feeling stressed, sad, or burned out
- Consider diabetes support groups
- Get adequate sleep (7-8 hours per night)
- Practice stress management (deep breathing, meditation, hobbies)

## When to Check Blood Sugar

| Timing | Why |
|--------|-----|
| Before breakfast (fasting) | Baseline morning level |
| Before meals | Guide medication and food choices |
| 2 hours after meals | See how food affects your sugar |
| Before bed | Ensure safe overnight levels |
| Before/during/after exercise | Prevent lows during activity |
| When you feel unwell | Detect highs or lows early |
| Before driving | Ensure safe blood sugar for driving |

## Managing Low Blood Sugar (Hypoglycemia)

### Signs (blood sugar <70 mg/dL)
Shakiness, sweating, rapid heartbeat, dizziness, hunger, irritability, confusion

### What to Do — Rule of 15
1. Eat 15 grams of fast-acting carbs:
   - 4 glucose tablets
   - 4 oz juice or regular soda
   - 1 tablespoon honey or sugar
2. Wait 15 minutes
3. Recheck blood sugar
4. Repeat if still <70 mg/dL
5. Eat a snack once blood sugar is normal

### When It's an Emergency
- Blood sugar <54 mg/dL
- Confusion, seizure, or loss of consciousness
- Unable to eat or drink
- Call emergency services — someone may need to give you glucagon

## Managing High Blood Sugar (Hyperglycemia)

### Signs (blood sugar >180 mg/dL)
Increased thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, blurry vision, headache

### What to Do
- Drink water to stay hydrated
- Take medications as prescribed
- If on insulin, follow your correction dose instructions
- Check for ketones if blood sugar >250 mg/dL (type 1)
- Light physical activity may help (if no ketones present)

### When to Call Your Doctor
- Blood sugar stays above 250 mg/dL for more than 2 readings
- Moderate to large ketones present
- You're vomiting and can't keep fluids down

### When It's an Emergency
- Blood sugar >300 mg/dL with symptoms
- Fruity breath, rapid breathing, severe nausea/vomiting
- Confusion or altered mental status
- Go to the emergency room immediately

## Sick Day Rules

When you're ill (cold, flu, infection, stomach bug):

1. **NEVER stop taking insulin** — you may need more, not less
2. Check blood sugar every 2-4 hours
3. Check ketones if blood sugar >250 mg/dL
4. Drink plenty of fluids (water, broth, sugar-free drinks)
5. Try to eat your normal meals; if you can't, try:
   - Crackers
   - Soup
   - Applesauce
   - Regular gelatin or popsicles (if not eating, you may need sugar)
6. Keep taking your diabetes medications
7. Hold SGLT-2 inhibitors during acute illness
8. Rest

### Call Your Doctor If:
- Blood sugar >250 mg/dL for more than 2 checks
- Moderate to large ketones
- Vomiting more than 4 hours
- Can't keep fluids down
- Fever over 101°F (38.3°C) lasting more than 24 hours
- Diarrhea lasting more than 6 hours

## Appointments to Keep

| Appointment | Frequency | Purpose |
|-------------|-----------|---------|
| Primary care / endocrinologist | Every 3-6 months | A1C, medication review, overall care |
| Eye exam (dilated) | Annually | Screen for retinopathy |
| Foot exam | At every visit + annual comprehensive | Screen for neuropathy and circulation |
| Dental | Every 6 months | Screen for gum disease |
| Kidney screening (urine + blood) | Annually | Screen for nephropathy |
| Cholesterol panel | Annually | Cardiovascular risk |
| Blood pressure | Every visit | Hypertension screening |

## Emergency Kit — Always Have Available

- Blood glucose meter and test strips
- Fast-acting glucose (tablets, juice boxes)
- Glucagon kit (if on insulin)
- Medical ID bracelet or card
- List of all medications
- Healthcare provider's phone number
- Healthy snacks

## Disclaimer

This guide provides general self-care information for educational purposes. It is not a substitute for personalized medical advice from your healthcare team. Always follow the specific plan your doctor has created for you.
