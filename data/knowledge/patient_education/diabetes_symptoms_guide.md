# Diabetes Symptoms — Patient Guide

Source: CDC, NIDDK, ADA, WHO

---

## How Do I Know If I Might Have Diabetes?

Diabetes affects how your body turns food into energy. When you eat, your body breaks food down into sugar (glucose). Insulin, a hormone made by your pancreas, helps glucose get into your cells for energy. With diabetes, your body either doesn't make enough insulin or can't use it effectively, causing glucose to build up in your blood.

---

## Common Warning Signs

### The Classic Triad (Most Important)
These three symptoms together are the hallmark of diabetes:

1. **Frequent urination (polyuria)** — Needing to urinate much more often than usual, especially at night. High blood sugar causes your kidneys to work overtime to filter excess glucose, pulling extra water from your body.

2. **Excessive thirst (polydipsia)** — Feeling very thirsty no matter how much you drink. As your body loses fluids through frequent urination, you become dehydrated and thirsty.

3. **Excessive hunger (polyphagia)** — Feeling hungry even after eating. When your cells can't get glucose for energy, your body signals that it needs more food.

### Other Common Symptoms
- **Unexplained weight loss** — Losing weight without trying, even though you may be eating more. Your body starts burning fat and muscle for energy when cells can't access glucose.
- **Fatigue** — Feeling very tired, weak, or lacking energy. Without glucose reaching your cells, your body doesn't have the fuel it needs.
- **Blurred vision** — High blood sugar can cause the lens of your eye to swell, affecting your ability to see clearly.
- **Slow-healing wounds** — Cuts, bruises, or sores that take longer than usual to heal.
- **Frequent infections** — Getting more infections than usual (urinary tract infections, yeast infections, skin infections).
- **Numbness or tingling** — Tingling, numbness, or pain in your hands or feet (more common in type 2 diabetes).
- **Dry, itchy skin** — Dehydration and poor circulation can cause skin problems.

## Symptoms by Diabetes Type

### Type 1 Diabetes
- Symptoms appear quickly — over days to weeks
- Often more severe at onset
- May first show up as DKA (see emergency symptoms below)
- Common in children and young adults
- In children: watch for bed-wetting or more bathroom trips

### Type 2 Diabetes
- Symptoms develop slowly — over months to years
- Many people have NO symptoms for a long time
- Often discovered during routine blood tests
- Additional signs:
  - Dark patches of skin around neck, armpits, or groin (acanthosis nigricans)
  - Slow-healing sores, especially on feet
  - Numbness or tingling in hands/feet

### Gestational Diabetes
- Usually has NO symptoms
- Detected through routine screening at 24-28 weeks of pregnancy
- If symptoms occur: mild increase in thirst or urination

## Emergency Warning Signs — Seek Immediate Help

Go to the emergency room or call emergency services immediately if you experience:

- **Fruity-smelling breath** — This is a sign of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA), a life-threatening condition
- **Nausea and vomiting** combined with high blood sugar
- **Confusion or difficulty thinking clearly**
- **Rapid, deep breathing** (Kussmaul breathing)
- **Loss of consciousness or fainting**
- **Seizures**
- **Severe abdominal pain** with nausea/vomiting
- **Chest pain** or pressure
- **Very high blood sugar** (>300 mg/dL) with symptoms
- **Very low blood sugar** (<54 mg/dL) that doesn't improve with treatment
- **Unable to keep fluids down** for more than 4 hours

## When to See a Doctor (Non-Emergency)

Make an appointment with your doctor if you notice:

- Any of the classic symptoms (excessive thirst, frequent urination, unexplained hunger)
- Unexplained weight loss
- Increasing fatigue that doesn't improve with rest
- Wounds that won't heal
- Frequent infections
- Numbness or tingling in extremities
- Vision changes
- A family member has diabetes and you haven't been screened

---

## Who Should Get Screened?

Even without symptoms, get tested if you:

- Are 35 years or older
- Are overweight and have any additional risk factor:
  - Family history of diabetes
  - Are African American, Hispanic/Latino, Native American, Asian American, or Pacific Islander
  - Have high blood pressure
  - Have a history of gestational diabetes
  - Have PCOS (polycystic ovary syndrome)
  - Are physically inactive
  - Have had abnormal blood sugar tests in the past

## Understanding Your Risk Level

| Situation | What It Means | What to Do |
|-----------|--------------|------------|
| No symptoms, no risk factors | Low risk | Routine screening at age 35+ |
| No symptoms, has risk factors | Moderate risk | Get screened — many people have diabetes without knowing |
| Mild symptoms (fatigue, thirst) | May be early diabetes | See your doctor for blood glucose testing |
| Multiple symptoms (thirst + urination + weight loss) | Likely elevated blood sugar | See your doctor promptly for testing |
| Emergency symptoms (confusion, fruity breath, vomiting + high sugar) | Possible DKA or HHS | Go to emergency room immediately |

## Important Disclaimer

This information is for educational purposes and triage support only. It is NOT a diagnosis. Only a qualified healthcare professional can diagnose diabetes through proper laboratory testing. If you are experiencing symptoms, please consult your doctor.
